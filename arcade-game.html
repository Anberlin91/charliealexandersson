Arcade Game

Designed a side-scroller game mechanic and complete game experience built under 3 weeks at Futuregames.

Overview

Creating an engaging arcade experience from scratch

This project challenged us to design and build a complete arcade-style side-scroller game within a tight 3-week timeframe. The goal was to create a game that felt immediately fun and intuitive, with mechanics that players could learn within seconds but continue to find satisfying over time.

As the UX designer on the team, the focus was on crafting the core interaction loop, ensuring responsive controls, clear visual feedback, and a difficulty curve that kept players engaged without frustrating them.

Problem

How do you make a game feel good to play in just 3 weeks?

Arcade games rely heavily on "game feel" — the tactile sensation of controls, timing, and feedback. With such a short development timeline, the challenge was to identify what made the biggest impact on player experience and prioritize ruthlessly.

Key questions included: What makes a side-scroller feel responsive? How do we communicate danger and progress without tutorials? How do we keep the scope manageable while delivering a complete experience?

  • Tight timeline requiring rapid iteration and decision-making
  • Need for intuitive controls without onboarding or tutorials
  • Balancing scope against a polished, complete experience
  • Creating satisfying feedback loops within minimal mechanics

Process

From paper prototypes to playable builds

The process started with rapid concept exploration. Paper prototypes helped the team align on core mechanics before any code was written. From there, a greybox prototype in Unity allowed early playtesting of movement, jumping, and collision interactions.

Iterative playtesting sessions with classmates revealed friction points — the initial jump arc felt floaty, enemy patterns were too predictable, and the health system lacked visual clarity. Each session informed the next design sprint.

Research

Analysed classic arcade side-scrollers to identify common UX patterns: screen-space usage, enemy telegraph timing, and power-up placement conventions.

Prototyping

Built low-fi paper prototypes for level layout, then moved to Unity greybox builds for testing movement feel and collision timing.

Solution

A focused side-scroller with satisfying game feel

The final design centred on three core principles: responsive controls, clear visual hierarchy, and progressive difficulty. Jump arcs were tightened based on playtester feedback. Enemy patterns were redesigned to telegraph attacks clearly, giving players the information they needed to react.

A minimalist HUD design communicated health, score, and progress without cluttering the screen. The colour palette used high-contrast accents to draw attention to interactive elements and hazards.

Final Game Screenshots / UI Mockups

Outcome

Lessons in speed, scope, and player focus

The project delivered a complete, playable arcade game within the 3-week deadline. Playtesting feedback confirmed that the controls felt responsive and the difficulty curve kept players engaged. The minimalist HUD received positive remarks for keeping focus on the gameplay.

Key takeaways included the importance of early prototyping, the value of frequent playtesting, and learning to cut features that didn't serve the core experience. The project reinforced that great game UX is about removing friction, not adding features.

  • Delivered a complete, polished game within the 3-week timeline
  • Positive playtester feedback on controls and game feel
  • Learned to prioritize core mechanics over feature breadth
  • Reinforced the value of rapid prototyping and iteration
